How to Sell a House with Code Violations in California

By Patrick Mahoney February 24, 2026 6 min read

Discovering that your California home has building code violations can feel like a roadblock standing between you and a successful sale. But thousands of homeowners across the state sell properties with violations every year — and you have more options than you might think. Understanding what code violations are, what you're legally required to disclose, and which selling path makes the most sense for your situation can save you significant time, money, and stress.

What Are Building Code Violations and How Do They Affect a Sale?

Building codes are the minimum safety and construction standards set by California state law and local municipalities. A code violation occurs when work on a property — whether done by you or a previous owner — doesn't meet those standards, or when improvements were made without the required permits.

Code violations can surface at several points in the sale process. A buyer's home inspector may flag unpermitted work. The buyer's lender may require code compliance before approving a mortgage. A city permit pull during the transaction can reveal open violations on record. Any of these discoveries can derail a deal, delay closing, or cause a buyer to renegotiate — or walk away entirely. That's why it's important to know what you're dealing with before you list.

Common Types of Code Violations in California

California's housing stock includes many older homes where work was done without permits, sometimes decades ago. The most frequently encountered violations include:

  • Unpermitted additions and conversions. Garage conversions, added bedrooms, sunrooms, and ADU (accessory dwelling unit) construction done without permits are extremely common in California. Square footage added without permits may not legally count toward the home's listed size.
  • Electrical issues. Outdated wiring (such as aluminum wiring or knob-and-tube), panels that don't meet current amperage requirements, and DIY electrical work that bypassed inspections all represent code violations that can concern lenders and buyers alike.
  • Plumbing deficiencies. Unpermitted plumbing additions, galvanized pipes past their service life, and improperly vented drain systems are common in homes built before the 1980s. Some violations also relate to water heater strapping requirements unique to California's seismic standards.
  • Structural concerns. Modifications to load-bearing walls, room additions without proper foundations, and decks or patios built without structural review all fall into this category. In earthquake-prone California, structural code compliance carries significant weight.
  • Zoning and land use violations. Operating an unpermitted rental unit, building too close to property lines, or exceeding lot coverage limits can all create complications during a sale.

California Disclosure Requirements

California law places strict disclosure obligations on sellers. Under Civil Code Section 1102 and related statutes, sellers must complete a Transfer Disclosure Statement (TDS) and a Seller Property Questionnaire (SPQ) that require you to disclose known material defects and any known code violations or unpermitted work.

The key word is known. You are legally required to disclose violations you are aware of — you are not required to hire an inspector to hunt for problems you don't know about. However, deliberately concealing a known violation can expose you to significant legal liability after the sale, including lawsuits for misrepresentation or fraud.

The safest approach: disclose what you know, in writing, before accepting any offer. A reputable cash buyer will factor disclosed violations directly into their offer rather than using them as a last-minute renegotiation tool.

Your Three Options for Selling a Home with Code Violations

Option 1: Fix the Violations Before Listing

If violations are minor and relatively inexpensive to remediate, bringing the property into compliance before listing can open the door to the broadest pool of buyers — including those using conventional financing, which often won't fund homes with known code issues.

The downside is cost and time. Retroactive permitting in California can be a lengthy process. Depending on the municipality, you may need to open walls for inspection, bring all related systems up to current code (not just the code at the time of original construction), and pay both permit fees and contractor costs. For significant violations, remediation can run tens of thousands of dollars and take months.

Best for: Homeowners with minor, low-cost violations who have time and available capital for repairs and aren't in a hurry to close.

Option 2: Price the Property Lower to Reflect the Violations

Some sellers choose to list the property on the open market at a reduced price, fully disclosing the violations and letting buyers factor the remediation cost into their offers. This can work, but it comes with risks. Many traditional buyers using mortgage financing will be blocked by lenders who won't fund properties with open violations. You may be limited to cash buyers or investors anyway — but paying a listing agent's commission (typically 5–6%) in the process.

Best for: Sellers with mild violations, plenty of time, and comfort navigating a potentially slow or complicated listing process.

Option 3: Sell As-Is to a Cash Buyer

Selling directly to a cash home buyer is often the fastest and most straightforward path when code violations are present. Cash buyers purchase properties in their current condition — violations, unpermitted work, deferred maintenance, and all — with no requirement for repairs before closing. There are no lender appraisals to satisfy, no contingencies tied to code compliance, and no risk of a deal falling through because a buyer's mortgage was denied.

Best for: Homeowners who need to sell quickly, want to avoid the cost and hassle of repairs, or whose violations would make traditional financing difficult to obtain.

Why Cash Buyers Are the Right Fit for Code Violation Properties

Traditional buyers — even those who are willing — face real obstacles when purchasing a home with code violations. Conventional lenders typically require properties to meet minimum safety and habitability standards before approving a loan. FHA and VA loans have even stricter requirements. This limits your buyer pool dramatically and can leave you waiting months for a deal that may still fall apart at the eleventh hour.

Cash buyers operate outside these constraints. Because no lender is involved, there's no appraisal contingency, no underwriting requirement, and no minimum property condition standard to satisfy. For many California homeowners dealing with code violations, the math favors the as-is cash sale even when the offer is below retail value.
