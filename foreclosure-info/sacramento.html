Foreclosure in Sacramento, CA — What You Need to Know

As the state capital, Sacramento's foreclosure court (the Sacramento County Superior Court) processes a large volume of trustee sale confirmations — which also means resources and timelines are well established. If you've received a Notice of Default or a Notice of Trustee Sale on your Sacramento home — or you've simply fallen behind — this page walks you through exactly what happens next and what you can do at each stage.

Every foreclosure in Sacramento follows California's non-judicial foreclosure process. That means there's a predictable timeline with clear legal rights at each step. The sooner you understand where you are in that timeline, the more options you have.

The 5 Stages of Foreclosure in California

California is a non-judicial foreclosure state, meaning most foreclosures happen outside of court on a predictable timeline. Understanding where you are in the process tells you exactly which options are still on the table.

Stage 1 — Missed Payments (Days 1–90)

Foreclosure starts the day you miss a mortgage payment. After 15 days, you'll typically owe a late fee. After 30 days, your servicer reports the late payment to credit bureaus. Between days 30 and 90, you'll receive calls, letters, and a formal "demand letter" or "breach letter." This is the cheapest and easiest stage to resolve. Lenders are legally required to make a "good faith" contact attempt 30 days before filing a Notice of Default.

Stage 2 — Notice of Default (NOD)

After roughly 90 days of missed payments, the lender (through a trustee) records a Notice of Default with the county recorder. This is a public document — you'll likely start receiving mail from foreclosure-rescue companies and attorneys the same week. The NOD triggers California's mandatory 90-day reinstatement period, during which you have the right to bring the loan fully current and stop the foreclosure.

Stage 3 — 90-Day Reinstatement / Cure Window

This is the most important window. For 90 days from the NOD recording, you can stop foreclosure entirely by paying the past-due amount plus fees. This is also the window where loan modifications, forbearance agreements, short sales, and cash sales are most realistic — because the trustee sale date hasn't been set yet, buyers and lenders still have time to act.

Stage 4 — Notice of Trustee Sale (NOS)

If the default isn't cured, the trustee records a Notice of Trustee Sale and publishes it for 3 consecutive weeks in a local newspaper and posts it on the property. The NOS can be recorded no earlier than 90 days after the NOD. The actual trustee sale is scheduled for at least 21 days after the NOS is first published. This means California's total minimum timeline from first NOD to trustee sale is roughly 111 days.

Stage 5 — Trustee Sale (Auction)

The property is auctioned to the highest bidder on the courthouse steps (or online). If no third party bids higher than the lender's opening bid, the lender takes the property back as "REO" (Real Estate Owned). Once the sale completes and the deed is recorded, your ownership ends. In most cases, any remaining equity beyond the loan balance is yours — but recovering it after a trustee sale is slow, legally complex, and rarely returns full value.

8 Ways to Stop or Relieve a Foreclosure

The right option depends on how far into the timeline you are, how much equity you have, and your financial situation. Each of these is worth exploring before the trustee sale date is set.

1. Reinstatement

Pay the past-due amount (principal, interest, fees, trustee costs) in a single payment. California law gives you the right to reinstate up to 5 business days before the trustee sale date. This stops foreclosure entirely and puts your loan back on its original terms.

2. Loan Modification

Your lender permanently changes the loan terms — usually a lower interest rate, a longer term, or both — to make payments affordable. Apply early in the 90-day NOD window. California's Homeowner Bill of Rights requires servicers to evaluate a complete modification application before proceeding with a trustee sale.

3. Forbearance

A short-term agreement that pauses or reduces payments for 3–12 months. Missed payments are added to the end of the loan or worked out in a repayment plan. Good for temporary hardship (job loss, medical event), not long-term affordability problems.

4. Refinance

Replace your current mortgage with a new one at better terms. Requires sufficient credit, income, and home equity — usually realistic only in the earliest stages of delinquency, before the NOD is recorded, since many refinance lenders won't fund during an active foreclosure.

5. Repayment Plan

Your servicer agrees to accept your regular monthly payment plus a portion of the past-due amount for a set number of months until you're caught up. Fastest to set up of all retention options; easiest to qualify for if your income has recovered.

6. Short Sale

Sell the property for less than the loan balance with the lender's written approval. Takes longer than a traditional sale (typically 60–120 days for lender approval alone) and creates a credit hit, but avoids the full foreclosure on your record. Only makes sense if you're underwater on the mortgage.

7. Sell to a Cash Buyer Before the Trustee Sale

If you have equity, selling to a cash buyer before the trustee sale date lets you cancel the foreclosure, pay off the loan, keep your remaining equity, and protect your credit from the most damaging marks. Redwood Home Buyers closes in as little as 7 days, which fits inside even late-stage foreclosure timelines. No repairs, no listing, no showings, no commissions.

8. Deed in Lieu of Foreclosure

Voluntarily transfer the deed to your lender in exchange for release from the debt. Less damaging to credit than a completed foreclosure but still a significant negative event. Realistic only when the loan balance roughly matches the home's value and the lender agrees in writing.

About Bankruptcy

Filing Chapter 7 or Chapter 13 bankruptcy triggers an automatic stay that halts any pending foreclosure sale. It's a legal pause button, not a solution — it buys time to work out one of the options above. Talk to a bankruptcy attorney before filing; the credit impact lasts 7–10 years.

What To Do Right Now — Step-by-Step

  1. Confirm where you are in the timeline. Pull any letters from your servicer. Look for "Notice of Default" or "Notice of Trustee Sale." Note every date.
  2. Open every letter from your lender. Ignoring mail is the most common and most expensive mistake homeowners make in foreclosure.
  3. Call your servicer's loss mitigation department. Ask directly about loan modification, forbearance, and repayment plans. Get the representative's name and a reference number.
  4. Contact a HUD-approved housing counselor — free. Call 1-800-569-4287 or visit HUD.gov/findacounselor. They are government-funded, cost nothing, and can sometimes negotiate with your lender on your behalf.
  5. Watch for foreclosure scams. No legitimate person will ask you to sign your deed over, pay upfront fees for a "guaranteed" modification, or make payments to anyone other than your servicer.
  6. Get an honest read on your equity. Subtract your total loan balance from a realistic home value. Equity determines which options make sense — modification for little/no equity, cash sale for significant equity.
  7. If you have equity and can't qualify for retention, sell before the trustee sale. Whether that's listing with an agent or selling to a cash buyer depends on how much time is left. Under 60 days, a traditional listing rarely closes in time.
  8. Document everything in writing. Every agreement with your lender — modification, forbearance, repayment plan — needs to be signed and dated. Verbal promises do not stop trustee sales.

California Foreclosure Resources

  • HUD-Approved Housing Counselors (free): HUD.gov/findacounselor or 1-800-569-4287.
  • California Homeowner Bill of Rights: oag.ca.gov/hbor — explains your legal protections during foreclosure.
  • Keep Your Home California / CalHFA Mortgage Relief: camortgagerelief.org — state-run mortgage assistance programs.
  • California Department of Financial Protection and Innovation: dfpi.ca.gov — report foreclosure scams or servicer misconduct.
  • Legal Aid: lawhelpca.org — free legal help for qualifying low-income homeowners.
