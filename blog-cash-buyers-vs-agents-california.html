Cash Home Buyers vs. Real Estate Agents: Which Is Right for You?

By Patrick Mahoney March 3, 2026 7 min read

If you're thinking about selling your California home, you've likely already encountered the two most common paths: listing with a licensed real estate agent or selling directly to a cash home buyer. At first glance, the choice seems straightforward — agents get you more money, cash buyers get you more speed. But the reality is far more nuanced. Commissions, repairs, staging, carrying costs, and your personal timeline all play a role in which option actually puts more money in your pocket and less stress on your plate.

This guide breaks down both options side by side — costs, timelines, ideal scenarios, and the myths that mislead sellers every day — so you can make an informed decision for your specific situation.

Option 1: Listing with a Real Estate Agent

The traditional home sale involves hiring a listing agent who markets your property on the MLS, coordinates showings, negotiates offers, and manages the transaction through closing. In California, listing agents typically charge 2.5–3% commission, and you'll also pay the buyer's agent commission of another 2.5–3%, bringing your total agent cost to 5–6% of the sale price.

For a $500,000 home, that's $25,000–$30,000 in commissions before you account for anything else. On top of that, most agent-listed homes require sellers to make repairs or upgrades to pass inspections, professionally stage the property, and keep it "show ready" for weeks or months. According to the National Association of Realtors, the average seller spends between $5,000 and $15,000 preparing a home before listing.

Then there's the timeline. Even in a strong California market, most agent-listed homes take 30–60 days to receive an accepted offer, followed by a 30–45 day escrow period for the buyer's financing to clear. From list date to close, you're typically looking at 60 to 90+ days — and that's when everything goes smoothly. Deals fall through when buyers can't secure financing, when inspections reveal unexpected issues, or when appraisals come in below the agreed price.

Option 2: Selling to a Cash Home Buyer

A cash home buyer is a company or individual investor who purchases properties directly with cash — no bank financing, no MLS listing, no open houses. You submit your property details, receive an offer (usually within 24 hours), and if you accept, you can close in as little as 7 to 14 days.

Because there's no lender involved, there's no appraisal contingency, no financing contingency, and no waiting on underwriters. Cash buyers also purchase homes as-is, meaning you don't repair a leaking roof, update outdated kitchens, or deep-clean before closing. There are no agent commissions to pay, and reputable cash buyers cover all closing costs.

The trade-off is that cash offers are typically below full market value — cash buyers need to account for their cost of repairs, holding costs, and profit margin. However, once you subtract agent commissions, repair costs, staging fees, carrying costs (mortgage, taxes, insurance, utilities for 3–6 months), and the risk of a deal falling through, the net difference between the two paths is often much smaller than sellers initially expect.

Full Cost Breakdown: Agent vs. Cash Buyer

Here's a realistic comparison for a $450,000 California home that needs moderate work:

Traditional Agent Sale

  • Agent commissions (5.5%): −$24,750
  • Pre-sale repairs and updates: −$10,000 to −$20,000
  • Staging and photography: −$2,000 to −$5,000
  • Seller-paid closing costs: −$3,000 to −$6,000
  • Carrying costs (4 months at ~$2,500/mo): −$10,000
  • Price reductions or concessions: −$5,000 to −$15,000
  • Estimated net proceeds: $370,000 to $400,000

Cash Buyer Sale

  • Cash offer (typically 80–90% of ARV): $360,000 to $405,000
  • Agent commissions: $0
  • Repairs: $0
  • Staging: $0
  • Closing costs (covered by buyer): $0
  • Carrying costs (closes in 7–14 days): ~$0
  • Estimated net proceeds: $360,000 to $405,000

In this scenario, the two options are often within $10,000–$30,000 of each other in net proceeds — and for many sellers, the certainty, speed, and simplicity of the cash sale more than justify the difference.

Timeline Comparison at a Glance

One of the most significant differences between the two options is time. Here's how the timelines typically compare in California:

  • Agent listing — pre-market prep: 2–6 weeks for repairs, staging, photography
  • Agent listing — time on market to accepted offer: 2–8 weeks
  • Agent listing — escrow and closing: 30–45 days
  • Agent listing — total typical timeline: 60 to 120+ days
  • Cash buyer — offer turnaround: 24 to 48 hours
  • Cash buyer — time to close: 7 to 14 days
  • Cash buyer — total typical timeline: 1 to 3 weeks

If your situation involves an urgent deadline — a job relocation, an impending foreclosure, a divorce settlement, or a probate proceeding — the 60–90+ day agent timeline may simply not be an option.

When a Real Estate Agent Is the Right Choice

Listing with an agent makes the most sense when you have time on your side and a home that will show well. Specifically, an agent is likely your better option if:

  • Your home is in excellent condition and requires little to no work before listing
  • You're in a high-demand California market where multiple-offer situations are common
  • You have 3–6 months to complete the sale without financial hardship
  • Maximizing sale price is your primary goal and you're willing to manage the process
  • You're comfortable with uncertainty — deals falling through, renegotiations, appraisal gaps

In short, if your home is move-in ready and the market is hot, an agent can generate competitive bids that a cash buyer simply cannot match. The process takes longer and involves more risk, but the payoff can be worth it for the right seller in the right circumstances.

When a Cash Buyer Is the Right Choice

A cash sale makes sense in a wide variety of circumstances that are more common than most homeowners realize. Consider selling to a cash buyer if any of these situations apply:

Facing Foreclosure

If you're behind on mortgage payments and your lender has issued a Notice of Default, the clock is ticking. California's foreclosure timeline can move quickly, and a cash sale can close fast enough to pay off your mortgage, protect your credit, and give you a fresh start — before the bank takes the property.

Inherited Property

Inheriting a home is both an emotional and logistical challenge. If the property needs work, is located far away, or you're sharing ownership with other heirs, a straightforward cash sale eliminates the complexity. You avoid managing contractors, navigating probate delays, and coordinating between multiple parties over months.

Property Needs Significant Repairs

Foundation issues, roof damage, outdated electrical, mold — these problems don't just reduce your sale price, they can make a traditional listing nearly impossible. Cash buyers purchase homes in any condition. You don't spend a dollar on repairs, and the buyer assumes all the risk and cost of bringing the property up to standard.

Relocating for Work or Personal Reasons

When you've accepted a job offer across the country and need to move in 30 days, a traditional 90-day sale isn't a realistic option. A cash sale lets you close on your schedule, avoid managing an empty property from across the state or country, and eliminate the double-carry of two housing costs.

Going Through Divorce

Divorce proceedings often require the marital home to be liquidated so proceeds can be divided. A fast cash sale simplifies this process, reduces the time both parties are financially entangled, and eliminates the tension of managing showings, negotiations, and repairs together during an already difficult time.

Landlords Tired of Problem Tenants

If you own a rental property with difficult tenants, deferred maintenance, or lease complications, selling on the traditional market is messy. Cash buyers routinely purchase tenant-occupied properties and handle the tenant situation themselves — so you can walk away without the legal and logistical headaches.

Common Myths About Cash Home Buyers — Debunked

Despite growing awareness, cash buyers are still surrounded by misconceptions that cause sellers to dismiss a legitimate option without fully evaluating it.

Myth 1: "Cash buyers always lowball you."

Cash offers are typically below full retail value — but so is your net proceeds from a traditional sale once you subtract commissions, repairs, and carrying costs. Reputable cash buyers explain their offer formula transparently. An offer that appears lower at first glance often compares very favorably once you account for the true cost of the alternative.

Myth 2: "It's only for desperate sellers."

Cash buyers serve a wide range of sellers — estate attorneys, corporate relocation programs, investors looking to exit rental portfolios, and homeowners who simply value speed and certainty over top-dollar. Choosing a cash sale is a strategic decision, not a last resort.

Myth 3: "Cash buyers are scammers."

While predatory buyers do exist, legitimate cash home buying companies are licensed, have verifiable business histories, and provide contracts you can have reviewed by a real estate attorney before signing. Warning signs of a scam include upfront fees, refusal to provide references, and pressure to sign without adequate review time. Established companies like Redwood Home Buyers are fully transparent about their process and never pressure sellers to accept an offer.

Myth 4: "You'll have to move out immediately."

Most cash buyers are flexible on closing dates and post-close occupancy. If you need a few extra weeks after closing to relocate, that's a simple conversation — not a deal-breaker.

Myth 5: "The process is risky or complicated."

A cash sale is actually simpler than a traditional sale. There are fewer contingencies, no lender requirements, and no appraisal hurdles. The contracts are shorter and the timeline is compressed, which means less time for things to go wrong.
